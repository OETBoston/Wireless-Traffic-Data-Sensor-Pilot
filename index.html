Curb & Traffic Data Sensor Pilot

Boston's Office of Emerging Technology is testing new privacy-first traffic data sensors to quantify how Bostonians use city streets and curbs

PILOT WINDOW: JULY 16–23, 2026
LOCATIONS: BACK BAY & DOWNTOWN

The City of Boston's Office of Emerging Technology (OET), working with Boston's Streets Cabinet, is testing a new generation of sensors to understand how curb space is used and how vehicles move through neighborhoods. OET's sensors detect wireless signals that vehicles and phones broadcast, allowing the City to measure how people use the street while preserving personal privacy (no cameras and no GPS location tracking). The one-week pilot covers two areas of the city: a three-block stretch of Boylston Street in Back Bay, and a six-block area along Washington Street and Milk Street downtown.

Why We're Doing This

City Hall can make better decisions about parking policy and traffic management when we can see how curb space and streets are actually used, block by block, hour by hour. Today, that picture is built piecemeal, through manual counts and spot checks. This pilot tests whether wireless sensors can fill that gap, quickly and affordably, without collecting personally-identifiable information.

01

Smarter Curb Management

Observed data on parking turnover and traffic movements helps the City create curb and signal policies that match how residents actually use the street.

02

More Insights on Congestion

Vehicle volume data helps identify where traffic backs up, and why, without waiting for a resident complaint.

03

A Foundation for Future Planning

Aggregated trends feed directly into Streets Cabinet decisions on curb policy, loading zones, and traffic safety.

🔒 Privacy-First Technology

Your Privacy Is Protected

Sensors detect wireless signals that phones and vehicles broadcast publicly. Observed device IDs are not personally identifiable, and every ID is irreversibly hashed before it is logged by our sensors. The City will not store, or have access to, raw IDs that trace back to a specific device.

This Technology Does NOT Collect

  • Photos, video, or audio
  • Names, addresses, or vehicle license plates
  • GPS coordinates or precise location tracking
  • Any link between this data and a specific person or device owner

This Technology DOES Collect

  • Hashed Bluetooth, WiFi, and TPMS device IDs (one-way, irreversible)
  • Timestamps and signal strength

Common Questions

Does this track my phone?

Sensors briefly detect the Bluetooth or WiFi signal your phone already broadcasts, the same signal it sends when deciding whether to connect to a speaker or car stereo, and convert it into an anonymous, one-way hash the instant it's detected. That anonymous hash is the only thing that ever reaches the City. Your phone's identity never does, and it isn't followed once you leave the test area.

What's new compared to last year's test?

Last year's pilot measured traffic using tire-pressure (TPMS) signals alone. This pilot builds on that same privacy-first foundation and goes further, adding Bluetooth and WiFi detection into the same sensor unit to give the City a fuller picture of both vehicle and pedestrian activity.

Can I opt out?

Turning off Bluetooth and WiFi, or setting your device to "not discoverable," ensures your device will not be detected - for this pilot, or by any other Bluetooth or WiFi connection.

Can this data be linked back to me?

Every device ID is hashed the instant it's detected. This one-way hash ensures all data is anonymous. We never connect IDs to a name, account, or registration database, and the City has built this pilot specifically so such tracking is not possible.

Data Retention & Security

  • Every device ID is irreversibly hashed before storage
  • Data is analyzed and shared only in aggregate form, never at the level of an individual device
  • Raw sensor data is deleted 60 days after the test ends
  • Access is restricted to authorized vendors and City of Boston personnel

All privacy-related technical documentation is available to the public upon request.
